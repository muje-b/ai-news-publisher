Synergizing Physically Constrained MCMC and Chemical-Informed Gaussian Processes for Reaction Network Discovery

When chemists try to uncover reaction mechanisms from limited, noisy measurements, they face a knotty problem: the hidden network of elementary steps (the reaction topology) and the numeric rate constants that drive them are entangled. A recent pre‑print introduces PC‑MCMC‑CIGP, a gray‑box workflow that deliberately separates these two layers while enforcing known physical laws. By coupling a stochastic topology sampler with conservation and thermodynamic checks, and then refining parameters with a chemistry‑aware Gaussian process, the method offers a transparent route from data to interpretable kinetic models.

What You Need to Know

PC‑MCMC‑CIGP stands for Physically Constrained Markov Chain Monte Carlo coupled with a Chemical‑Informed Gaussian Process. The workflow begins with a spike‑and‑slab prior that treats each possible elementary reaction as either present (spike) or absent (slab). An MCMC sampler explores this discrete space, proposing additions or removals of reactions while calculating the posterior probability of each topology.

Before a proposed network is accepted, the algorithm applies hard constraints: elemental mass balance must be satisfied for every species, and detailed balance or other thermodynamic feasibility checks are enforced. Only topologies that pass these screens move on to the parameter‑estimation stage.

There, a Chemical‑Informed Gaussian Process (CIGP) models the residual between the observed time‑series and the deterministic kinetics implied by the current network. The CIGP incorporates known functional forms (e.g., Arrhenius temperature dependence) as kernels, allowing it to capture systematic model error while providing uncertainty estimates. These uncertainties drive an acquisition function that selects the next experiment most likely to reduce ambiguity in both topology and rate constants.

Why It Matters

Many data‑driven discovery tools treat reactions as black‑box functions, producing accurate forecasts but offering little mechanistic insight. PC‑MCMC‑CIGP forces the model to respect conservation laws and thermodynamic principles, which are non‑negotiable in chemistry. The result is a sparse, interpretable network that can be directly linked to reaction pathways, making it easier for experimentalists to design follow‑up tests or for theorists to connect the findings to quantum‑chemical calculations.

Beyond interpretability, the explicit uncertainty quantification helps prioritize resources. Instead of running a dense grid of experiments, the workflow points to measurements that will most efficiently prune impossible topologies or tighten rate‑constant estimates. This active‑learning loop can accelerate discovery in fields where each experiment is costly or time‑consuming, such as catalysis, atmospheric chemistry, or biochemical pathway elucidation.

Key Details

  • Spike‑and‑slab topology sampler: binary inclusion variables for each candidate reaction, explored via MCMC with proposal moves that add, remove, or swap reactions.
  • Hard physical constraints: elemental balance equations are enforced exactly; thermodynamic feasibility (e.g., Wegscheider conditions) is checked before accepting a topology.
  • Chemical‑Informed Gaussian Process: kernel combines a standard RBF component with a parametric mean function derived from Arrhenius law, allowing the GP to learn systematic deviations while honoring known temperature dependence.
  • Uncertainty‑aware acquisition: expected information gain (or variance reduction) computed from the joint posterior over topology and parameters guides experimental design.
  • Benchmark on H₂ + Br₂: the constrained sampler correctly identified the known chain‑branching mechanism and rejected spurious pathways that unconstrained methods frequently retained.
  • Reproducibility: all code, priors, and synthetic data are released with a detailed README, enabling other groups to plug in their own reaction libraries or measurement models.

What's Next

The authors plan to extend PC‑MCMC‑CIGP to multi‑temperature datasets and to incorporate heterogeneous catalysis descriptors directly into the CIGP kernel. They also aim to test the workflow on real‑world systems such as combustion of hydrocarbons and enzyme‑catalyzed networks, where sparse data and strong physical priors are both essential. By tightening the loop between theory, computation, and experiment, the approach could become a standard tool for anyone seeking mechanistic models from limited chemical time‑series.
