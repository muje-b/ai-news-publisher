Midjourney goes from generating cat images to full-body ultrasound scans

When most people think of Midjourney, they picture whimsical AI‑generated cats lounging in surreal landscapes. The company’s CEO, David Holz, recently shifted the conversation from feline art to a piece of hardware that looks more like a medical device than a creativity tool.

What You Need to Know

Holz unveiled a prototype called The Midjourney Scanner, a full‑body ultrasound system built around a circular array of transducers. The ring emits high‑frequency sound waves and records the echoes as a person stands inside, producing a series of vertical slices that are stitched together into a three‑dimensional image of the body’s interior.

The scanner does not rely on ionizing radiation; it uses the same physics that powers prenatal ultrasounds, but with a denser sensor layout to capture deeper tissues and finer detail. Holz said the device is intended for wellness and preventive health checks, offering a non‑invasive way to view muscle thickness, fat distribution, and vascular patterns without a trip to a hospital.

Why It Matters

Midjourney’s move into hardware signals a broader ambition to combine its AI expertise with physical sensing technology. By feeding the raw ultrasound data into its existing generative models, the company hopes to produce clearer, more interpretable scans that can highlight anomalies or track changes over time.

For consumers, the scanner could become a routine part of personal health monitoring, similar to how wearable heart‑rate monitors are used today. For clinicians, a low‑cost, radiation‑free imaging option could expand access to early detection, especially in underserved areas where traditional MRI or CT scanners are scarce.

Key Details

  • The scanner’s sensor ring contains 64 ultrasonic transducers spaced evenly around the torso.
  • Operating frequency ranges from 5 MHz to 15 MHz, allowing penetration up to 20 cm with sub‑millimeter resolution.
  • Software reconstructs the raw echo data into a 3D volume in under 30 seconds using a pipeline similar to Midjourney’s image‑generation workflow.
  • Initial testing focused on measuring muscle cross‑sectional area and subcutaneous fat layers with accuracy comparable to clinical ultrasound.
  • Holz announced plans to open a wellness spa in San Francisco where members can book scanner sessions and receive AI‑generated health reports.
  • The hardware is still in prototype stage; no regulatory clearance has been obtained yet, and the company is seeking partnerships with medical device firms for further validation.

What's Next

Midjourney will refine the scanner’s hardware and software, pursue FDA or CE marking, and begin pilot studies with health professionals. If those trials succeed, the company aims to launch the scanner commercially within the next two years, positioning it as a bridge between its AI‑driven imaging roots and tangible, health‑focused products.
