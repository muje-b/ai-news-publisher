OpenAI’s Jalapeño chip is Big Tech’s spiciest move away from Nvidia

OpenAI disclosed that it is developing a custom inference accelerator named Jalapeño in partnership with Broadcom. The chip is intended to run the company’s large language models more efficiently than relying solely on third‑party GPUs. This effort places OpenAI alongside Google, Apple, and SpaceX, which have all pursued proprietary silicon to lessen dependence on a single supplier.

What You Need to Know

The Jalapeño chip is an application‑specific integrated circuit (ASIC) designed for inference workloads. OpenAI says the chip will be fabricated on a 5‑nanometer process node, a node currently used by many leading AI accelerators. Broadcom is providing the physical design and packaging expertise, while OpenAI contributes the architecture tailored to its transformer‑based models.

Unlike training chips, Jalapeño focuses on the inference phase, where models generate responses after being trained. OpenAI expects the chip to reduce latency and lower the cost per token served in its API offerings. The company plans to integrate Jalapeño into its existing inference stack gradually, beginning with internal testing later this year.

Why It Matters

Nvidia’s GPUs have dominated the AI hardware market, giving the company considerable pricing power and creating a supply‑chain single point of failure for many AI developers. By fielding its own inference silicon, OpenAI aims to mitigate that risk and gain more control over performance characteristics and cost structures for its services.

The move also signals a broader trend where large AI players are vertically integrating hardware to match their software ambitions. As more companies design custom chips, the market may see increased competition, which could drive innovation and potentially lower barriers for smaller organizations seeking efficient inference solutions.

Key Details

  • Chip name: Jalapeño
  • Partner: Broadcom (design and manufacturing)
  • Target workload: inference of large language models
  • Process technology: 5‑nanometer node
  • Expected benefits: lower latency, reduced cost per token
  • Rollout plan: internal testing later 2025, gradual integration into API

What's Next

OpenAI will likely share performance benchmarks and power‑efficiency figures once the first silicon samples are validated. If Jalapeño meets its projected targets, the company may expand its use to other model families and consider offering the chip to external customers through its cloud services. The broader industry will watch how this shift affects Nvidia’s market share and whether it accelerates the diversification of AI hardware suppliers.
