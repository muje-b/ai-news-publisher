Can Language Model Agents be Helpful Circuit Explainers in Mechanistic Interpretability?

Mechanistic interpretability aims to peek inside neural networks and understand why they make certain predictions. Researchers have become good at finding the parts of a model that matter—what we call circuits—but turning those raw components into clear, human‑readable explanations still takes a lot of manual work. A new paper asks whether a language model, acting as an autonomous agent, can shoulder part of that explanatory burden once a circuit has already been isolated.

What You Need to Know

The study introduces AgenticInterpBench, a benchmark comprising 84 semi‑synthetic transformer circuits. Each circuit comes with component‑level annotations—163 in total—that describe what individual neurons, attention heads, or MLPs are supposed to do. This gives evaluators a concrete ground truth for judging how well an explainer can interpret a piece of the model.

On top of the benchmark, the authors propose HyVE (Hypothesize, Validate, Explain). HyVE is an agentic loop: it first observes activation patterns of a target component, then generates a natural‑language hypothesis about its function, designs a causal intervention (e.g., ablation or activation patching) to test that hypothesis, and finally refines or expands the explanation based on the outcome. The process repeats until the agent settles on a stable description, which it outputs both for the component and, when aggregated, for the whole circuit.

Why It Matters

Explaining circuits manually is time‑consuming and prone to inconsistency across researchers. If a language model agent can reliably produce accurate component descriptions, it could free up human experts to focus on higher‑level synthesis or on circuits that resist automated explanation. Moreover, a standardized agentic explainer could be integrated into interpretability pipelines, making results more reproducible across labs.

The benchmark also addresses a current gap: most interpretability evaluations focus on whether a method can locate important parts, not on how well it can articulate what those parts do. By providing a shared set of circuits with annotated functions, AgenticInterpBench lets the community compare different explanation strategies on equal footing.

Key Details

  • Benchmark size: 84 semi‑synthetic transformer circuits, 163 component‑level annotations.
  • HyVE loop: Observe → Hypothesize → Validate (causal test) → Explain, iterated until convergence.
  • Evaluation metric: Accuracy of component‑level explanations against the benchmark annotations, measured with exact match and token‑level F1.
  • Baseline comparison: HyVE outperforms a simple prompting baseline (LLM asked to explain without validation) by ~22% F1.
  • Failure analysis: Most errors stem from ambiguous hypotheses that the causal test cannot disambiguate, suggesting a need for richer intervention designs.
  • Resource cost: Running HyVE on a single component averages ~0.8 GPU‑seconds, making it feasible for large‑scale benchmarking.

What's Next

Future work could extend AgenticInterpBench to include more diverse architectures (e.g., multimodal transformers) and real‑world models trained on natural language data. Improving the hypothesis generation module—perhaps by integrating retrieval of relevant literature or using chain‑of‑thought prompting—may reduce the ambiguity that currently limits HyVE’s performance. Finally, combining agentic explanations with human-in-the‑loop verification could yield a hybrid approach that scales interpretability without sacrificing rigor.
