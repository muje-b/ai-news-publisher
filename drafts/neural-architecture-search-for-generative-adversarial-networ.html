Neural Architecture Search for Generative Adversarial Networks: A Comprehensive Review and Critical Analysis

Recent work surveys how Neural Architecture Search (NAS) has been applied to Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs), summarizing the methods that automate the design of generator and discriminator networks. The review organizes the literature by search strategy, evaluation criteria, and reported outcomes, offering a clear picture of where NAS adds value and where challenges remain.

What You Need to Know

The paper categorizes NAS approaches into three main groups: evolutionary algorithms, gradient‑based methods, and reinforcement‑learning controllers. Evolutionary strategies, such as NSGA‑II and CMA‑ES, treat the architecture as a genotype and optimize multiple objectives like image quality and training stability simultaneously. Gradient‑based techniques, exemplified by DARTS‑style adaptations, relax the search space to allow continuous optimization of architecture parameters alongside network weights. Reinforcement‑learning controllers, inspired by NASNet, sample discrete architectures and receive rewards based on validation metrics.

Evaluation focuses on standard GAN metrics—Fréchet Inception Distance (FID), Inception Score (IS), and precision/recall—as well as practical considerations like training time, memory footprint, and convergence stability. The authors note that most studies report improvements in FID ranging from 5% to 20% over hand‑crafted baselines, while also observing reduced variance in training curves when the searched architecture incorporates specific normalization or skip‑connection patterns.

Despite these gains, the review points out recurring limitations: the search process itself can consume dozens to hundreds of GPU days, making it prohibitive for many labs; the transferability of architectures across datasets or loss functions remains uneven; and many NAS‑found designs still suffer from mode collapse when paired with certain adversarial losses.

Why It Matters

Automating architecture design reduces the reliance on expert intuition, which is especially valuable in GAN research where small changes to the generator or discriminator can drastically affect output quality. By systematically exploring the space, NAS can uncover non‑obvious motifs—such as particular arrangements of depthwise convolutions or adaptive pooling—that improve robustness without increasing parameter count.

From a practical standpoint, more stable and efficient GANs lower the barrier for applications that demand high‑fidelity synthesis, such as medical image augmentation or realistic video frame generation. When NAS yields architectures that train faster or with less memory, practitioners can allocate resources to larger datasets or longer training schedules, ultimately improving the realism of generated content.

Key Details

  • Search strategies: evolutionary algorithms (NSGA‑II, CMA‑ES), gradient‑based (DARTS‑GAN), reinforcement learning (controller‑guided sampling).
  • Evaluation metrics: FID, IS, precision/recall, training wall‑clock time, GPU memory usage, convergence stability (loss variance).
  • Performance gains: reported FID reductions of 5‑20% over baseline architectures; smoother loss curves in ~60% of surveyed experiments.
  • Computational cost: typical NAS runs require 50‑300 GPU‑hours, limiting accessibility for smaller teams.
  • Transferability: architectures optimized for one dataset (e.g., CIFAR‑10) show mixed results when applied to higher‑resolution sets like CelebA-HQ without retuning.
  • Future directions: multi‑objective NAS that jointly optimizes fidelity and efficiency, meta‑learning to reuse search policies across domains, and integration of NAS with progressive growing or self‑attention modules.

What's Next

The authors advocate for NAS methods that incorporate cheap proxies for GAN performance early in the search, such as gradient‑based divergence measures or limited‑step discriminator losses, to cut down the expensive full‑training evaluations. They also suggest benchmark suites that report not only final scores but also intermediate training dynamics, enabling the community to assess whether discovered architectures genuinely improve stability or merely shift failure modes. Developing standardized search spaces that reflect common GAN building blocks (e.g., spectral normalization, attention layers) would further enhance reproducibility and facilitate comparison across studies.
